My Better Half

11/07/2014

back to blog index

While I have worked on many collaborative projects, pairing is really unique in that the collaboration is happening on the same screen on the same piece of code. Now that I'm used to it though, I am impressed by its value, whether its brainstorming ideas, having a second set of eyes to check code, etc. It really can open up a whole new level of problem-solving.

The best pairing, I've seen so far is that where the "roles" seem effortless. In the very beginning of the process we were bonked over the head with a somewhat rigid idea of what a Navigator (the overseer and guide of the code) and a Driver (the poor bastard doing the typing while also adding feedback… sorta) were. And while I understand that we had to start somewhere, and that was really as good a place as any, the most successful pairing sessions I've had are those in which we assigned the roles, but there was a line of communication that transcended them. With that came the freedom to switch roles for a second if it made sense. This rigid system can give way to a really vibrant collaboration, but you have to start with the system to guarantee that you understand your partners needs.

The pairings that feel less successful are those in which you feel confined in whatever role to which you've been assigned. Suddenly you're withholding information, not maliciously, but because that's not your role. That's not really constructive in a two-person collaboration. Otherwise if that "transcendent" communication isn't there, then you might become lost.

A great discussion we had in our accountability group today was about when you are in a pairing and you need to step away from the code. Was it appropriate to in the pairing? Would it offend your pair? We ultimately agreed that if you need a second away from the code or a moment of silence, youâ€™ve got to ask for it—politely of course.

The feedback aspect thus far seems watered down. I have no delusions that I am the perfect pair, but all of my feedback has been very "nice." I've started trying to do 5 minutes of feedback at the end of pairing sessions, because I feel like that leads to a more honest and ACTIONABLE conversation. The important thing with feedback is to know that it is a gift offered to help you—not a barb.

Giving feedback has been relatively comfortable for me, but I definitely feel myself soft-selling it because I don't want to offend people that I barely know. I think that's probably fine for now and will improve as these relationships progress. Like I mentioned, I've started to rely on more in person feedback/check–ins at the end of pairings as I think I can usually give more actionable feedback in the context of a conversation than a few hundred characters. Of course it's nice to also type it out later in the feedbackinator as a nice summary of your discussion.
